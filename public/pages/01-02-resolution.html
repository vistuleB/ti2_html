Let $G=(V,E)$ be a graph and $s : V \rightarrow \{0,1\}$ the parity requirements. We can encode each constraint $c_u: \sum_{e \sim v} x_e$ as a CNF formula. This creates $2^{d(v)-1}$ clauses, each of size $d(v)$. Let us denote this CNF formula by $F_u(G,s)$ or simply $F_u$ if $G$ and $s$ are understood from the context. We finally define

\begin{align*} \tseitin(G,s) := \bigwedge_{u \in V} F_u(G,s) \ . \end{align*}

If $G$ has maximum degree $k$ then this gives us a $k$-CNF formula with $|E|$ variables and at most $2^{k-1} |V|$ clauses. If $G$ is $k$-regular then $\tseitin(G,s)$ has exactly $2^{k-1} |V|$ clauses, each of width exactly $k$.

If an odd number of vertices have a parity requirement of $1$, then $\tseitin(G,s)$ is unsatisfiable and we can reason about resolution refutations of it.

Resolution

When two clauses $C, D$ contain opposing literals, i.e., when $C = C' \vee x$ and $D = D' \vee \bar{x}$ then $C \vee D$ is called a resolvent of $C$ and $D$. The variable $x$ is called the resolved variable. We write a resolvent rather than the resolvent since there might be multiple resolvents: for example when $C = (x \vee y \vee z)$ and $D = (\bar{x} \vee \bar{y} \vee w)$ then both $(y \vee z \vee \bar{y} \vee w)$ and $(x \vee z \vee \bar{x} \vee w)$ are resolvents; in this case of course both resolvents are trivial: they always evaluate to $1$. We observe: if two clauses have a non-trivial resolvent then this is their unique resolvent; and if two clauses have a unique resolvent then it is also non-trivial.

Definition (Resolution Proof). Let $F$ be a CNF formula. A resolution proof from $F$ is a sequence

\begin{align*} C_1, C_2, \dots, C_l \end{align*}

of clauses such that for each $1 \leq i \leq l$

  1. $C_i$ is a clause of $F$ or
  2. There are earlier clauses $C_{j_1}, C_{j_2}$ with $j_1, j_2 \lt i$ such that $C_i$ is a resolvent of $C_{j_1}, C_{j_2}$.

If $C_l = \Box$ then the proof is called a resolution refutation of $F$. The size of the proof is $l$. The width of the proof is $\max \{|C_i| \ | \ 1 \leq i \leq l \}$.

Suppose $C$ is a clause appearing in a resolution proof from $F$. Then $F$ logically implies $C$, denoted by $F \Rightarrow C$, meaning that for every assignment $\alpha \in \{0,1\}^V$ with $F(\alpha) = 1$ also $C(\alpha)=1$. Maybe $C$ is already implied by a subset of clauses of $F$. The following question turns out to be important:

What is a minimal set $G$ of clauses of $F$ that still implies $C$?

In the case of a Tseitin formulas $F = \tseitin(G,s)$, the clauses of $F$ come in bundles $F_u(G,s)$, our encoding of the constraint $c_u: \sum_{e \sim v} x_e \equiv s(v)$. In this case, the following slightly different question turns out to be better:

What is a minimal set $S \subseteq V$ of vertices such that \begin{align*} F_S := \bigwedge_{v \in S} F_v(G,s) \end{align*} implies $C$?

The second question is different insofar as we still ask for a subset of clauses of $F$ implying $C$, but we enforce an all-or-nothing policy for our bundles $F_u$: either take all clauses of $F_u$ or none. The second question is important because it ties the boundary $\delta(S)$ of a set to the width of a clause:

Lemma Let $C$ be a clause implied by $F$ and let $S \subseteq V$ be a minimal subset such that $F_S$ implies $C$. Then $\delta(S) \subseteq \var(C)$: all edges leaving $S$ appear in $C$ as variables (negated or not).

Proof. Let $e \in \delta(S)$. This means that $e = \{u,w\}$ with $u \in S$ and $w \not \in S$. We have to show that $x_e$ or $\bar{x}_e$ appears in $C$.

Since $S$ is a minimal set of constraints implying $C$, the set $S \setminus \{u\}$ does not imply $C$. What does it mean not to imply somthing? It means that there is an assignment $\alpha$ to the variables such that

\begin{align*} F_{S \setminus \{u\}} (\alpha) = 1 \textnormal{ but } C(\alpha) = 0 \ . \end{align*}

So $\alpha$ satisfies all constraints $c_v$ for $v \in S \setminus \{u\}$. But it violates $c_u$. Why? Suppose $\alpha$ did satisfy $c_u$ as well; then it would satisfy all of $F_S$. By choice of $S$ we know that $F_S$ implies $C$ so $C(\alpha)=1$, a contradiction. We conclude:

\begin{align*} \textnormal{for every $v \in S$: } \quad F_v(\alpha) = \begin{cases} 0 & \textnormal{ if $v=u$, } \\ 1 & \textnormal{ if $v \in S \setminus \{u\}$.} \end{cases} \end{align*}

We don't know about $F_v(\alpha)$ for $v \in V \setminus S$ but we don't care, either. Suppose we take $\alpha$ and flip the value it assigns to edge $f$. We get a new assignment $\beta$ defined by

\begin{align*} \beta(x_e) := \begin{cases} \neg \alpha(x_e) & \textnormal{ if $e = f$, } \\ \alpha(x_e) & \textnormal{ if $e \ne f$.} \end{cases} \end{align*} Now consider some $v \in S \setminus \{v\}$. The edge $f = \{u,w\}$ is not incident to $v$ and thus $x_f$ does not appear in $c_v$ and neither in $F_v$, its equivalent CNF formula. This means that $F_v(\beta)=F_v(\alpha)=1$. What about $c_u$? The assignment $\alpha$ violates $c_u$, as seen above: $\sum_{e \sim u} \alpha(x_e) \ne s(u)$. This sum also includes the edge $f$; thus, when we consider $\sum_{e \sim u} \beta(x_e)$, the two sums differ in exactly one term, namely that of $x_f$, and thus the latter has a different parity, and thus $\sum_{e \sim u} \beta(x_e) \ne \sum_{e \sim u} \alpha(x_e) \ne s(u)$. This means that $\beta$ satisfies $c_u$. So $F_S(\beta)=1$; since $F_S$ implies $C$ we also know that $C(\beta)=1$. To summarize: \begin{align*} C(\alpha) & = 0 \\ C(\beta) & = 1 \ . \end{align*}

How can that be? How can $C$, violated by $\alpha$, suddenly be satisfied by $\beta$? The two assignments differ only in $x_f$, so this can only happens if $x_f \in \var(C)$. \(\square\)

Now if $F = \tseitin(G,s)$ is unsatisfiable and we could somehow show that every resolution refutation of $F$ contains a clause $C$ for which this minimal implying set $S$ has a large boundary $\delta(S)$, then we could show that $C$ must be large, too. In other words: we could show that every resolution refutation of $F$ has large width.

Resolution width lower bounds from expansion

Definition Let $h \in \R$ and $t \in \N$. A graph $G=(V,E)$ is a $(t,h)$-expander if

\begin{align} \label{eqn-expansion} |\delta(S)| \geq h \cdot |S| \end{align}

for every set $S \subseteq V$ of size $|S| \leq t$.

Typically, we say that $G$ is a good expander if it is $k$-regular for constant $k$ and $t \in \Omega(n)$ and $h \in \Omega(k)$. I don't want to make this formal right now.

Example. If $G = (V,E)$ is $d$-regular and has girth at least $g$, i.e., if every cycle of $G$ has length at least $g$, then $G$ is a $(g-1,d-2)$-expander.

Why? Well, take a set $S \subseteq V$ with $|S| \leq g-1$. The induced graph $H = G[S] = (S,E_S)$ must be acyclic, i.e., a forest. A forest has fewer edges than vertices, so $|E_S| \leq |E|-1$. Let's sum up the degrees of the $u \in S$. Since $G$ is $d$-regular, this is $d|S|$. Expanding the sum differently, we get the following (the notation $u \sim v$ means that $\{u,v\} \in E$, and the expression $[u \sim v]$ is $1$ if $u \sim v$ and $0$ if $u \not \sim v$):

\begin{align*} d |S| & = \sum_{u \in S} \deg_G(u) \\ & = \sum_{u \in S} \left( \sum_{v \in S} [u \sim v] + \sum_{v \in V \setminus S} [u \sim v] \right) \\ & = \sum_{u \in S} \sum_{v \in S} [u \sim v] + \sum_{u \in S}\sum_{v \in V \setminus S} [u \sim v]\\ & = 2 |E_S| + |\delta(S)| \\ & \leq 2 (|S|-1) + \delta(S) \leq 2 |S| + \delta(S) \ . \end{align*}

In words: the edges included in $S$ or leaving $S$ generate $d|S|$ "endpoints". At most $2 |E_S|$ of them can be inside $G[S]$. The others must be on an edge leaving $S$. We conclude that $\delta(S) \geq (d-2) |S|$.

Theorem (Good expanders give hard Tseitin formulas). Let $F = \tseitin(G,s)$ be an unsatisfiable Tseitin formula. If $G=(V,E)$ is connected and a $(t,h)$-expander then every resolution refutation of $F$ has width at least $th/2$.

Proof. Recall that for a clause $C$ implied by $F$ we considered a minimal set $S \subseteq V$ such that $F_S(G,s)$ implies $C$. Let's formally define a complexity measure for $C$:

\begin{align*} \mu(C) := \min \{|S| \ | S \subseteq V \textnormal{ and } F_S \textnormal{ implies } C \} \ . \end{align*}

First we claim that $\mu(\Box) = |V|$. Indeed, $F_V = F = \tseitin(G,s)$ implies $\Box$ (since it is unsatisfiable) so $V$ can take the role of $S$ in the definition of $\mu$. But are we sure there is no smaller such $S$? Suppose $S \subsetneq V$. So there is a vertex $u \not \in S$. Let's change the parity requirement of $u$ to obtain new requirements $s'$:

\begin{align*} s'(v) & = \begin{cases} \neg s(v) & \textnormal{ if $v = u$} \\ s(v) & \textnormal{ if $v \ne u$.} \end{cases} \end{align*}

Since $G$ is connected and the number of vertices with parity requirement $1$ is even in $s'$, the formula $\tseitin(G,s')$ is satisfiable. Since all other parity requirements are the same, the formula $F_S$ is also a subformula of $\tseitin(G,s')$ and thus $F_S$ is satisfiable. Therefore $F_S$ cannot imply $\Box$. We conclude that $\mu(\Box) = |V|$.

Now consider a resolution refutation of $F$ and a clause $C$ in it. If $C$ is the resolvent of two earlier clauses $D_1$ and $D_2$, how are $\mu(C), \mu(D_1), \mu(D_2)$ related? If $S_1 \subseteq V$ is a smallest set such that $F_{S_1}$ implies $D_1$, and $S_2 \subseteq V$ is a smallest set such that that $F_{S_2}$ implies $D_2$, then clearly $F_{S_1} \wedge F_{S_2}$ implies $D_1 \wedge D_2$. Therefore it implies $C$. We conclude: for $S := S_1 \cup S_2$ it holds that $F_S$ implies $C$. Therefore $\mu(C) \leq |S| \leq |S_1| + |S_2| = \mu(C_1) + \mu(C_2)$. This implies in particular that $\max ( \mu(C_1), \mu(C_2)) \geq \mu(C) / 2$. Let's call $C_1, C_2$ the predecessors of $C$. It holds that every $C$ in the refutation is either

  1. a clause of $F$ itself; in this case we have $\mu(C) = 1$, or
  2. has a predecessor $D$ with $\mu(D) \geq \mu(C)/2$.

Thus, if we start at $\Box$ and trace back its predecessors, we can make sure that $\mu(C)$ never drops by more than a factor $1/2$. It starts at $|V|$ and ends at $1$, so surely at some point it falls in the interval $[t/2, t]$. At this point we have a clause $C$ in the refutation with $t/2 \leq \mu(C) \leq t$. This is often called a clause of intermediate complexity.

Let $S \subseteq V$ be a smallest set such that $F_S$ implies $C$, i.e., $|S| = \mu(C)$. Since $|S| \leq t$ we can apply that $G$ is a $(t,h)$-expander and conclude that

\begin{align*} |\delta(S)| \geq h\cdot |S| \geq ht/2 \ . \end{align*}

Together with this implies that $|C| \geq ht/2$. \(\square\)
